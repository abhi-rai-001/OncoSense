# 🧬 OncoSense: Cancer Drug Response Predictor

Welcome to **OncoSense**! This project is a web-based application designed to help researchers, students, and medical professionals understand how different cancer cell lines respond to various anti-cancer drugs. 

This document explains what the project does, the technical prerequisites, core definitions, and potential Viva (oral exam) questions to prepare you for your final year presentation.

---

## 🌟 What is OncoSense?
Finding the right treatment for cancer is incredibly difficult because every tumor is genetically unique. A drug that works perfectly for one patient might completely fail for another. 

**OncoSense** acts as an intelligent virtual laboratory. Instead of testing drugs on human patients or in expensive petri dishes, OncoSense uses pre-calculated data (generated by a machine learning model called CaDRReS) to **predict** whether a specific cancer cell line will be sensitive (destroyed by) or resistant (unaffected by) a specific drug.

### Key Features
1. **The Predictor Dashboard**: Select a cancer cell line and a drug. The app tells you if the cell line is resistant or sensitive, alongside interactive visual charts.
2. **AI Clinical Insights**: The app integrates with **Google Gemini (AI)** to read the prediction and explain *why* the drug works or fails on a biological level, acting like a virtual senior oncologist.
3. **Combination Therapies**: In the real world, doctors use multiple drugs at once to fight cancer. OncoSense calculates the mathematical "best pairs" of drugs for your selected cancer and uses AI to explain the medical rationale for combining them.
4. **The Data Explorer**: A massive, interactive, colorful heatmap that lets you zoom in and out of thousands of data points to spot macro-trends across the entire dataset.

## 📚 Core Definitions (In Detail)

Before diving into the code, it's important to understand the biological and mathematical terminology used in this project:

### 1. IC50 (Half-Maximal Inhibitory Concentration)
**What it is:** In pharmacology, IC50 is the standard measure of a drug's effectiveness. It literally means "How much of this drug (concentration) is needed to inhibit the biological process or kill 50% of the cancer cells in a sample?"
**How to interpret it:** 
- A **High IC50** means you need a massive amount of the drug to kill the cancer cells. This indicates the cancer is **resistant** to the drug.
- A **Low IC50** means you only need a tiny drop of the drug to kill the cancer cells. This indicates the cancer is highly **sensitive** to the drug.
- *(Note on our data: In the specific CaDRReS dataset used in OncoSense, the values were transformed into a "Sensitivity Score". Therefore, a HIGHER score in our system means the drug is MORE effective).*

### 2. CaDRReS (Cancer Drug Response prediction using a Recommender System)
**What it is:** CaDRReS is a state-of-the-art Machine Learning framework developed by bioinformatics researchers. 
**How it works:** Think of it like Netflix's recommendation algorithm, but for cancer. Instead of predicting what movies a user will like based on their history, CaDRReS predicts how a specific cancer cell line will react to a specific drug based on its genetic mutation profile. It uses a technique called "Matrix Factorization" to find hidden patterns between the molecular features of the cancer cells and the chemical properties of the drugs. 
**Our Usage:** OncoSense does *not* run the heavy CaDRReS model live. Instead, we use the massive dataset of pre-calculated predictions generated by the CaDRReS algorithm to power our lightning-fast web dashboard.

### 3. The Dataset (CCLE & GDSC)
**Where the data comes from:** The data powering OncoSense originates from two of the most important biological databases in the world:
1. **CCLE (Cancer Cell Line Encyclopedia)**: A project by the Broad Institute (MIT & Harvard) that provides public access to the genomic data (DNA mutations, RNA expression) of over 1,000 human cancer cell lines. This is the biological "input" data.
2. **GDSC (Genomics of Drug Sensitivity in Cancer)**: A massive database maintained by the Sanger Institute that contains physical experiments showing how those cell lines reacted to hundreds of anti-cancer drugs.
**The Final CSV:** The `CCLE_predicted_IC50.csv` file used in this project is the mathematical marriage of these two databases. The CaDRReS model looked at the genetics (from CCLE) and the physical experiments (from GDSC) to predict the IC50 scores for every possible combination.

## ⚙️ How Does It Work Under the Hood?

OncoSense uses a **Three-Tier Architecture**:

### 1. The Data (The Brains)
At the core of the app is a massive spreadsheet (`CCLE_predicted_IC50.csv`). This file contains the predicted IC50 scores for thousands of cell lines against various drugs.

### 2. The Backend (The Engine)
The engine of the app is built using **Python** and **Flask**.
- **Flask**: A lightweight web server. When you click a button in your browser, Flask receives the request, processes the data, and sends the webpage back to you.
- **Pandas**: A data analysis tool. When you ask for a prediction, Flask uses Pandas to quickly sift through the 100MB+ spreadsheet to find the exact IC50 score you requested in milliseconds.
- **Google Gemini API**: Flask talks to Google's AI servers via HTTP requests to generate human-readable clinical explanations.

### 3. The Frontend (The Face)
The interface you interact with in the browser is built with **HTML, CSS, and JavaScript**.
- **Vanilla CSS**: We bypassed heavy frameworks (like Tailwind) for a custom, raw CSS "Design System" to ensure the site remains incredibly fast and tailored.
- **Plotly.js**: A scientific graphing library used to draw the interactive bar charts, donut charts, and the massive data heatmap.
- **Glassmorphism**: The UI uses modern design techniques (frosted glass effects, subtle animations, dark themes) to make it feel like a premium, state-of-the-art medical tool.

## 💻 Prerequisites & Setup

To run this project locally, you need the following installed on your machine:
1. **Python 3.8+**
2. **Pip** (Python package installer)
3. A **Google Gemini API Key** (Generate for free at Google AI Studio)

### Installation Steps:
1. Clone the repository and navigate to the `flaskApp` folder.
2. Create a virtual environment: `python3 -m venv .venv`
3. Activate the environment: `source .venv/bin/activate` (Mac/Linux) or `.venv\Scripts\activate` (Windows)
4. Install dependencies: `pip install -r requirements.txt` (or install Flask, Pandas, google-generativeai, python-dotenv manually).
5. Create a `.env` file in the `flaskApp` folder and add your API key:
   ```env
   GEMINI_API_KEY="your-api-key-here"
   ```
6. Run the server: `python app.py`
7. Open your browser and navigate to `http://127.0.0.1:5000`

## 🎓 Expected Viva (Oral Exam) Questions

If you are presenting this for your Final Year Project, your professors will likely ask the following questions. Here is how to answer them:

### Q1: Why did you use Flask instead of Django or Node.js?
**Answer**: Flask is a lightweight micro-framework for Python. Because our core data processing relies heavily on Python's **Pandas** library (to parse the large biological CSV dataset), using a Python backend was necessary. Django is too heavy and comes with a built-in database ORM which we don't need since our data is static. Flask provided the perfect balance of speed, simplicity, and Python integration.

### Q2: How are you managing the massive 100MB+ CSV file? Doesn't it slow down the server?
**Answer**: We use the **Pandas** library to load the CSV file into a `DataFrame` in the server's RAM *once* when the application starts (`app.py`). By setting the "Cell Lines" as the dataframe's index, looking up a specific prediction takes milliseconds `O(1)` time complexity. We avoid reading the file from the hard drive on every user request.

### Q3: What is the role of the Generative AI (Gemini) in your project? Is it making the predictions?
**Answer**: No, Gemini is **not** making the predictions. The IC50 predictions were pre-calculated using a deterministic machine learning model (CaDRReS). Gemini is used strictly for **Explainable AI (XAI)**. It takes the deterministic mathematical result and translates it into a clinical rationale, explaining the biological mechanism of action so that a medical professional can understand the *why* behind the numbers.

### Q4: How does the Drug Combination Recommender work mathematically?
**Answer**: When a user selects a cell line, the backend uses Pandas to sort all 19 drugs by their predicted sensitivity (IC50 score) in descending order. It takes the top 3 most sensitive drugs and mathematically combines them into 3 distinct pairs (Top 1 + Top 2, Top 1 + Top 3, Top 2 + Top 3). It then sends these specific pairs to the Gemini API to request a rationale on why their biological pathways might act synergistically for the given cancer tissue.

### Q5: You are using Plotly.js for charts. Why not use Chart.js or D3.js?
**Answer**: Plotly is explicitly designed for scientific data visualization. It easily handles large-scale heatmaps with thousands of data points without lagging the browser. It also comes with built-in interactive features like zoom, pan, and hover tooltips out-of-the-box, which are essential for navigating dense bioinformatics data.

### Q6: What happens if the Gemini API goes down or the user's internet drops?
**Answer**: The application is built with fault tolerance on the frontend. If the `fetch()` call to the `/api/explain` or `/api/combinations` endpoint fails, or if it takes too long, the UI gracefully catches the error and displays a localized "AI Insights unavailable" message without breaking the rest of the application or the Plotly charts.

## 🔬 Advanced Viva Questions

### Q7: Explain the Hill Equation used in your Dose-Response Curve.
**Answer**: The Hill equation models how biological systems respond to a drug dose. It is expressed as:

```
Effect = (C^n) / (IC50^n + C^n)
```

Where:
- **C** = drug concentration at a given point
- **IC50** = the half-maximal inhibitory concentration (predicted by CaDRReS)
- **n** = the Hill coefficient controlling steepness of the sigmoid (we use n = 1.5, a standard pharmacological assumption)
- **Effect** = fraction of cells inhibited (0-1, displayed as 0-100%)

The equation produces a characteristic **S-shaped sigmoid curve**. At low concentrations the effect is minimal; it rises sharply around the IC50 point, then plateaus near 100%. This is the most widely used pharmacodynamic model in oncology and drug discovery.

### Q8: What is Matrix Factorization and how does CaDRReS use it?
**Answer**: Matrix Factorization decomposes a large, sparse matrix into two smaller, dense matrices of **latent features**. In CaDRReS:
- The original matrix has **cell lines as rows** and **drugs as columns**. Many cells are empty (missing experimental data).
- CaDRReS learns two latent matrices: one representing hidden "drug properties" and one for hidden "cell line properties".
- The **dot product** of corresponding rows and columns from these two matrices predicts the missing IC50 values.

It is essentially the same algorithm used by Netflix for movie recommendations — users are to cell lines as movies are to drugs as ratings are to IC50 scores. CaDRReS extends this with biological feature regression using gene expression data from CCLE.

### Q9: What is the difference between In Vitro and In Vivo predictions? Does OncoSense do both?
**Answer**:
- **In Vitro** ("in glass") = experiments done *outside* a living organism — in a test tube or petri dish. The CCLE/GDSC data used by CaDRReS is entirely in vitro.
- **In Vivo** ("in the living") = experiments done *inside* a living organism — animal models or human clinical trials.

**OncoSense is an in vitro prediction tool.** This is a critical limitation — in vitro results do not always translate to in vivo responses because real tumors have a 3D microenvironment, immune system interactions, and genetic heterogeneity that a simple cell line cannot replicate. OncoSense is designed for **research use only**, not clinical treatment decisions.

### Q10: Your application uses a static pre-computed CSV. What are the limitations and how would you fix them?
**Answer**:

**Limitations:**
1. **Frozen data**: Predictions only cover drugs/cell lines in the original training data. New drugs cannot be predicted without retraining.
2. **Memory footprint**: Loading the entire CSV into RAM on every Flask worker is wasteful in a multi-worker production setup.
3. **No personalization**: Real patient-specific genomic data differs significantly from standardized cell-line data.

**Proposed Fixes:**
1. Replace the static CSV with a **PostgreSQL or SQLite database** via SQLAlchemy for efficient query-based lookups.
2. Integrate the live CaDRReS inference script so users can **upload their own genomic profile CSV** for real-time predictions.
3. Add a **Redis caching layer** so the DataFrame is loaded once and shared across all worker processes.

### Q11: Explain your Docker setup. What problem does containerization solve?
**Answer**: The `Dockerfile` packages the entire application into a self-contained image. **The core problem it solves** is the classic *"it works on my machine"* issue — without Docker, the correct Python version, all pip packages, and exact file paths must be configured identically on every machine.

Docker bundles the Python runtime, dependencies, app code, and data CSV into a single portable image that runs identically on any OS. In production, this image is deployed to a cloud platform (AWS, GCP, or Render.com) and started as a container exposed on port 5000.

### Q12: What is Gunicorn and why is it used instead of Flask's built-in server?
**Answer**: Flask's built-in development server (`app.run(debug=True)`) is **single-threaded** — it can only handle one HTTP request at a time, making it unsuitable for production.

**Gunicorn** (Green Unicorn) is a production-grade WSGI HTTP server that spawns **multiple worker processes** (typically `2 x CPU cores + 1`) to handle concurrent requests in parallel. It is battle-tested for stability under real traffic loads and works seamlessly with Flask since Flask apps are WSGI-compliant by default. The `requirements.txt` includes gunicorn, and the Dockerfile uses it as the default entry point.

### Q13: What is Explainable AI (XAI) and how is it implemented in OncoSense?
**Answer**: Explainable AI refers to methods that make AI/ML model outputs understandable to humans. Standard ML gives you a number but cannot tell you *why*.

**OncoSense implements XAI in two layers:**
1. **Algorithmic transparency**: The CaDRReS output (the IC50 score) is deterministic and traceable via Matrix Factorization. The heatmap, dose-response curve, box plot, and scatter charts make the raw data visually explorable and interpretable.
2. **Generative AI narration**: The Gemini 2.5 Flash model acts as an "explainer". Given the IC50, drug name, cell line tissue type, and sensitivity verdict, it generates a clinical rationale describing the drug's mechanism of action and the biological reasons for resistance or sensitivity.

This two-layer approach (quantitative transparency + natural language explanation) is a best practice in medical AI applications.

### Q14: What are Latent Features in the context of CaDRReS?
**Answer**: "Latent" means **hidden or inferred, not directly observable**. CaDRReS discovers latent features by decomposing the drug-response matrix:

- **Drug latent features** might abstractly encode properties like "EGFR inhibitory potential" or "targets mitosis" — even though the model was never explicitly told those properties.
- **Cell line latent features** might abstractly encode "degree of EGFR pathway activation" or "p53 mutation status".

The dot product of these two latent vectors predicts the drug response. Using a small number of latent dimensions (e.g., 10) acts as a regularizer — forcing the model to find a compressed, meaningful biological representation and preventing overfitting to noise.

---

### Q15: How does the application handle Overfitting?
**Answer**: Overfitting is a risk in genomics because there can be far more features (20,000+ genes) than training samples (~1,000 cell lines). CaDRReS addresses this through:
1. **Dimensionality reduction** via latent features — compressing the genetic profile into ~10 dimensions forces generalization.
2. **L2 Regularization** — penalizes large weights in the latent matrices, preventing them from fitting to noise.
3. **Cross-validation on GDSC data** — the model was validated against held-out cell line/drug pairs not seen during training, confirming generalizability.

In the web application layer, overfitting is not applicable since we perform pure inference (lookup), not training.

### Q16: What is Pharmacogenomics and how does OncoSense apply it?
**Answer**: **Pharmacogenomics** is the study of how an individual's genetic makeup affects their response to drugs — the intersection of pharmacology and genomics.

In OncoSense, every prediction is fundamentally pharmacogenomic. The cell line's genomic features (gene expression profiles from CCLE) determine drug effectiveness:
- Cell lines with an activating **EGFR mutation** are typically sensitive to Erlotinib (EGFR inhibitor).
- Cell lines with **KRAS mutations** are typically resistant to Erlotinib because KRAS is downstream of EGFR and constitutively active regardless of EGFR inhibition.

OncoSense makes this relationship actionable by predicting sensitivity for specific genetic profiles against specific chemical compounds.

### Q17: Explain how your frontend handles state without React or Vue.
**Answer**: OncoSense uses **Vanilla JavaScript** deliberately — no framework overhead. State is managed through:
1. **Module-scoped variables** (`let selectedCellLine = ''`, `let selectedDrug = ''`) storing current selections.
2. **An embedded Flask data JSON block** (`<script id="flask-data" type="application/json">`) rendered by Jinja2 at page-load time. JavaScript reads this once via `JSON.parse()`.
3. **DOM manipulation** for UI updates (toggling CSS classes like `.open`, `.visible`).
4. **Async IIFEs** (Immediately Invoked Function Expressions) that fire `fetch()` calls to the AI APIs automatically on page load.

This eliminates JavaScript framework overhead while delivering a fully dynamic, reactive user experience.

### Q18: Why is the default sensitivity threshold 3.0 and can users change it?
**Answer**: The default of **3.0** was chosen based on the scale of CaDRReS sensitivity scores, which typically range from ~1.0 (very resistant) to ~8.0 (very sensitive). 3.0 provides a statistically meaningful lower-bound cutoff that separates the resistant tail of the distribution.

**Yes — users can change it.** Both the Predictor page and Data Explorer have an interactive slider (range: 0-10, step: 0.5). Changing it recalculates the sensitivity/resistance verdict, all statistics, and chart colours dynamically, allowing researchers to apply their own domain-specific clinical cutoffs.

### Q19: What security concerns exist and how do you address them?
**Answer**:
1. **API Key exposure**: Stored in `.env`, loaded via `python-dotenv`, never hardcoded in source, excluded from version control via `.gitignore`.
2. **Prompt Injection**: Users select from a pre-defined dropdown (not free text), so there is no direct injection vector into AI prompts. Inputs are validated server-side.
3. **Path Traversal**: The CSV path is computed using `os.path.join` and `os.path.dirname(__file__)`, preventing directory traversal attacks.
4. **No HTTPS/Auth**: As a research tool, the app currently lacks user authentication. A production clinical deployment would require HTTPS (TLS), OAuth2 or JWT-based authentication, and role-based access control.

### Q20: What is Collaborative Filtering and how does it relate to CaDRReS?
**Answer**: **Collaborative Filtering** is the broader machine learning paradigm behind recommendation systems. It works on the principle: *"If item A and item B are similar, and user X likes item A, they probably also like item B."*

Applied to CaDRReS: *"If Cell Line A and Cell Line B have similar gene expression profiles (i.e., they are genetically similar), and Drug X is effective against Cell Line A, then Drug X is likely also effective against Cell Line B."* The model "collaboratively" infers missing drug responses by finding patterns across all known (cell line, drug) pairs. This is why CaDRReS can predict responses for cell lines or drugs not fully characterized in the original experiments.

### Q21: Explain the architecture of the Dose-Response Curve chart. What libraries and data are used?
**Answer**: The Dose-Response Curve is rendered entirely on the **frontend using Plotly.js 2.27**, with no additional backend API call. The data comes from the Flask-injected JSON block (`flask-data`) already embedded in the HTML at render time — specifically `currentIC50`, `drug`, `threshold`, and `sensitivity`.

The JavaScript:
1. Generates 200 concentration points from near-zero to `3 x max(IC50, threshold)`.
2. Applies the Hill equation to each point to calculate % inhibition.
3. Builds four Plotly traces: a filled area for visual context, the main sigmoid line, a gold IC50 marker at 50% effect, and a purple threshold marker.
4. Adds SVG `shapes` for the vertical dashed IC50 line and horizontal 50% guide line.

The chart is fully interactive — users can hover for exact values, zoom in on the inflection point, and download it as a PNG.

### Q23: What is the CCLE dataset and what does it contain?
**Answer**: **CCLE** stands for the **Cancer Cell Line Encyclopedia**, a public resource developed by the Broad Institute (MIT & Harvard). It contains:

1. **Genomic data**: Whole-exome sequencing (mutations), copy number variation, RNA expression (transcriptomics), proteomics, and methylation data for 1,000+ human cancer cell lines.
2. **Drug sensitivity data**: IC50 measurements from physical experiments exposing cell lines to hundreds of anti-cancer compounds.
3. **Metadata**: Tissue of origin, cancer subtype, and histology for each cell line.

In OncoSense, CCLE provides the **gene expression input features** that CaDRReS uses to learn the latent cell-line representation. Cell line names like `MCF7_BREAST` follow CCLE's naming convention: `CELL_NAME_TISSUE_TYPE`.

### Q25: How is synergy defined in the Drug Combination Recommender?
**Answer**: In our implementation, synergy is defined **mathematically by sorting** rather than by a formal synergy score (like Bliss Independence or Loewe Additivity).

The backend sorts all 19 drugs by their individual IC50 sensitivity score for the selected cell line. The top 3 highest-sensitivity drugs are taken as the "most likely to work" candidates. These are combined as pairs, and the assumption is: *"If both drugs individually attack the cancer cell effectively via different biological pathways, combining them creates a multi-front attack that reduces the likelihood of resistance."*

The **Gemini AI** then evaluates whether the two drugs genuinely target complementary pathways (e.g., one targets EGFR, the other targets downstream MAPK) to provide a biologically reasoned synergy rationale — going beyond pure mathematical ranking.

A true production implementation would use the **Bliss Independence Model**: `Effect(A+B) = Effect(A) + Effect(B) - Effect(A) x Effect(B)`, where any result exceeding this expected additive effect is classified as true synergy.
